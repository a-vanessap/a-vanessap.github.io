Pediatrician shortage leaves rural Puerto Rico behind

Most doctors are concentrated in the San Juan metro area, leaving children in rural towns with limited access to care

By Amanda Pérez Pintado

At least 15 towns in Puerto Rico have no pediatricians, according to an analysis of Health Resources and Services Administration data, highlighting a growing crisis in children's healthcare access on the Caribbean archipelago.

Most general pediatricians are clustered in a few urban centers, especially the Metro area, which includes San Juan, Guaynabo and Carolina. San Juan alone has 241 pediatricians— more than one-quarter of all pediatricians on the island— as of 2022, the latest data available on HRSA's 2023-2024 Area Health Resource File, published earlier this year.

When adjusted for Puerto Rico's median child population (ages 0 to 19), Guaynabo ranks as the city with the most pediatricians available, with 26 doctors per 6,278 children, followed by San Juan, Mayagüez and Ponce.

Because of the lack of pediatricians in their area, some families have turned to general practitioners, who aren't specifically trained to treat children, said pediatrician and researcher Gredia Huerta.

The HRSA data, however, may not accurately reflect the actual number of pediatricians actively working on the island. While the agency's data shows roughly 850 general pediatricians in Puerto Rico in 2022, the Puerto Rico College of Physicians and Surgeons places the number closer to 500.

“Some pediatricians keep their medical licenses, but aren't actively practicing,” Huerta said.

In 2023, the Puerto Rico College of Physicians and Surgeons warned that the pediatrician shortage is deepening. According to the organization, many of the pediatricians who are active on the archipelago are approaching retirement age. A recent study commissioned by the Financial Oversight and Management Board for Puerto Rico found that more than half of pediatricians are over the age of 60.

The problem isn't new. A 2011 study by the Puerto Rican Pediatric Society already showed a trend of declining pediatrician numbers and identified low reimbursement rates from health insurance plans as one of the biggest barriers, according to local reports.

Puerto Rico faces an exodus of medical professionals, having lost over 8,600 doctors out of approximately 18,800 in just over a decade, according to a 2023 report by The Center for a New Economy. The mass migration contributes to a health care crisis marked by a shortage of specialists and long wait times to receive care.

“Physicians who choose to specialize in pediatrics do so out of a genuine interest and love for working with children and their families at all stages of their lives to ensure their well-being,” Huerta said. “It's crucial to encourage future generations of pediatricians to remain in Puerto Rico, and for that to happen, it's urgent to address and improve the current employment situation for pediatricians.”
